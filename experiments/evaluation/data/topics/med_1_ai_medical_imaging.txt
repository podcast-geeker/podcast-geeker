Artificial intelligence has emerged as one of the most transformative technologies in diagnostic radiology, promising to augment the capabilities of clinicians by automating the detection and characterization of abnormalities in medical images. The field of medical imaging generates an enormous volume of data — a single hospital may produce tens of thousands of radiographs, computed tomography scans, and magnetic resonance images each day — and the interpretation of these images remains a cognitively demanding task subject to human fatigue, perceptual error, and inter-reader variability. Against this backdrop, deep learning methods, particularly convolutional neural networks and, more recently, vision transformers, have demonstrated remarkable performance on a range of diagnostic tasks, from identifying pneumonia on chest radiographs to detecting diabetic retinopathy in fundus photographs.

Convolutional neural networks have formed the backbone of AI-assisted medical imaging since the mid-2010s. Architectures such as ResNet and DenseNet have proven especially effective for chest radiograph interpretation. ResNet introduced residual connections that allow gradients to flow through very deep networks, enabling the training of models with over one hundred layers without the degradation problem that plagued earlier architectures. DenseNet extended this idea by connecting each layer to every other layer in a feed-forward fashion, encouraging feature reuse and reducing the number of parameters needed to achieve competitive performance. These architectures were central to CheXNet, a landmark system developed at Stanford in 2017 that achieved radiologist-level performance in detecting pneumonia on frontal chest X-rays using a DenseNet-121 backbone trained on the ChestX-ray14 dataset, which contained over one hundred thousand images labeled for fourteen thoracic pathologies. CheXNet demonstrated that, under controlled experimental conditions, a deep learning model could match or exceed the sensitivity of board-certified radiologists for specific findings.

More recently, vision transformers have attracted considerable attention in the medical imaging community. Unlike CNNs, which build up spatial hierarchies through local convolution operations, vision transformers divide an image into fixed-size patches, embed each patch as a token, and process the entire sequence through self-attention layers. This architecture captures global context from the earliest layers of the network, which is particularly advantageous in medical imaging where pathological findings may be diffuse or may require integration of information from distant regions of an image — for example, assessing cardiomegaly requires understanding the ratio of heart size to thoracic width across the full radiograph. Hybrid architectures that combine convolutional feature extraction with transformer-based attention have shown promising results on tasks such as lung nodule classification and multi-label thoracic disease detection, often outperforming purely convolutional baselines.

The evaluation of AI systems in medical imaging relies on well-established statistical metrics. Sensitivity, also known as the true positive rate or recall, measures the proportion of actual positive cases that the model correctly identifies; a system with high sensitivity will miss few true abnormalities. Specificity, or the true negative rate, measures the proportion of actual negative cases correctly classified as normal; high specificity means fewer false alarms. These two metrics are often in tension — increasing sensitivity typically decreases specificity and vice versa — and their trade-off is visualized through the receiver operating characteristic curve, which plots sensitivity against one minus specificity at various classification thresholds. The area under the ROC curve provides a single scalar summary of discriminative performance, with a value of one representing perfect classification and 0.5 representing chance. In clinical practice, the choice of operating point on the ROC curve reflects the relative costs of missed diagnoses versus unnecessary follow-up investigations.

Performance comparison studies between AI systems and radiologists have yielded nuanced results. While headline findings from studies such as CheXNet and the CheXpert competition suggest parity or superiority of AI models, these comparisons are sensitive to study design, reader expertise, dataset composition, and the availability of clinical context. Radiologists in practice integrate imaging findings with patient history, prior examinations, and clinical presentation — information that is typically unavailable to AI models evaluated in isolation. Nevertheless, several large-scale reader studies have confirmed that AI-assisted interpretation can improve both sensitivity and efficiency, particularly in high-volume screening settings such as tuberculosis detection in resource-limited environments and mammographic breast cancer screening.

A critical concern with AI in medical imaging is the risk of underdiagnosis. False negatives — cases where the AI system fails to flag a genuine abnormality — carry potentially severe consequences, including delayed treatment, disease progression, and adverse patient outcomes. In screening programs, where the prevalence of disease is low, even a small decrease in sensitivity can translate into a meaningful number of missed cases at the population level. This raises important questions about liability: when an AI system is used as a second reader or triage tool and a diagnosis is missed, the allocation of responsibility among the AI developer, the deploying institution, and the interpreting clinician remains legally unsettled in most jurisdictions.

Bias in medical imaging AI represents another significant challenge. Training datasets frequently underrepresent certain demographic groups, including patients from racial and ethnic minorities, elderly populations, and individuals with atypical body habitus. Models trained predominantly on data from one population may perform poorly when deployed in a different clinical environment, a phenomenon known as dataset shift or domain shift. Studies have documented disparities in AI performance across racial groups for chest radiograph interpretation, raising concerns that deployment of biased systems could exacerbate existing health inequities. Addressing this issue requires deliberate curation of diverse and representative training datasets, rigorous subgroup analysis during model evaluation, and ongoing monitoring of performance after deployment.

The regulatory pathway for AI-based medical imaging devices in the United States is governed by the Food and Drug Administration. Most AI imaging tools have been cleared through the 510(k) pathway, which requires demonstrating that the new device is substantially equivalent to a legally marketed predicate device. For truly novel AI applications without a clear predicate, the De Novo classification pathway provides an alternative route that establishes a new regulatory category. The FDA has also introduced a framework for modifications to AI-based software as a medical device, acknowledging that these systems may be updated continuously as new training data become available. As of 2024, the FDA had authorized over seven hundred AI-enabled medical devices, the majority in radiology. Notable examples include IDx-DR, the first FDA-authorized autonomous AI diagnostic system, which detects greater-than-mild diabetic retinopathy in retinal images without requiring physician interpretation, and Viz.ai, which uses deep learning to detect suspected large vessel occlusion strokes on CT angiography and automatically alerts the neurointerventional team, significantly reducing time to treatment.

Integrating AI tools into clinical radiology workflows presents practical challenges beyond algorithmic performance. Radiologists must trust the system's outputs, which requires transparency regarding model confidence, failure modes, and the populations on which the system was validated. Workflow integration demands seamless interoperability with picture archiving and communication systems and electronic health records. Institutions must also address the computational infrastructure required to run inference in real time, ensure compliance with data privacy regulations such as HIPAA, and establish governance structures for monitoring system performance over time. The successful adoption of AI in medical imaging will ultimately depend not only on the technical sophistication of the algorithms but also on the thoughtful design of human-AI collaboration frameworks, robust regulatory oversight, and a sustained commitment to equity in the development and deployment of these powerful diagnostic tools.
