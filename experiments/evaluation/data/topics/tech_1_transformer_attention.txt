The Transformer architecture, introduced by Vaswani et al. in their landmark 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need," fundamentally reshaped the landscape of deep learning for sequence modeling. Prior to the Transformer, recurrent neural networks such as LSTMs and GRUs dominated tasks in natural language processing, machine translation, and sequence-to-sequence generation. These models processed tokens sequentially, maintaining hidden states that carried contextual information forward through time. While effective, this sequential dependency imposed severe limitations on parallelism during training and made it difficult to capture long-range dependencies in lengthy sequences. The Transformer dispensed with recurrence entirely, relying instead on a mechanism called self-attention to model relationships between all positions in a sequence simultaneously. This architectural choice unlocked massive gains in training efficiency and representational power, setting the stage for the era of large-scale pretrained language models.

At the heart of the Transformer lies the self-attention mechanism, which computes a weighted representation of an input sequence by allowing each token to attend to every other token. The process begins by projecting the input embeddings into three distinct vector spaces through learned linear transformations, producing what are known as queries, keys, and values. For a given token, its query vector represents what information it is looking for, its key vector represents what information it offers to other tokens, and its value vector contains the actual content that will be propagated forward. Attention scores are computed by taking the dot product of a query with all keys in the sequence, which yields a raw similarity score for each pair of positions. These scores determine how much each token should attend to every other token in the sequence.

The raw dot-product scores, however, can grow large in magnitude when the dimensionality of the key vectors is high. Large dot products push the softmax function into regions where its gradients become extremely small, effectively stalling learning. To address this, Vaswani et al. introduced scaled dot-product attention, in which the dot products are divided by the square root of the key dimension before the softmax is applied. Formally, for a matrix of queries Q, keys K, and values V, the attention output is computed as Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QK^T / sqrt(d_k)) V, where d_k is the dimensionality of the key vectors. This scaling ensures that the variance of the dot products remains controlled regardless of the embedding dimension, preserving healthy gradient flow and stabilizing training. The resulting softmax distribution assigns a normalized weight to each value vector, and the output for each position is a weighted sum of all value vectors, effectively blending information from across the entire sequence according to learned relevance.

A single attention function, however, captures only one type of relationship between tokens. In natural language, words relate to one another in many ways simultaneously—syntactically, semantically, coreferencially, and pragmatically. To enable the model to jointly attend to information from different representational subspaces, the Transformer employs multi-head attention. Rather than performing a single attention computation with full-dimensional queries, keys, and values, the model splits these vectors into multiple smaller heads, each operating on a reduced-dimensional subspace. Each head independently computes scaled dot-product attention, learning to capture a distinct type of relationship. The outputs of all heads are then concatenated and passed through a final linear projection to produce the combined multi-head attention output. In the original Transformer, eight attention heads were used with a model dimension of 512, giving each head a subspace of 64 dimensions. This design incurs no additional computational cost relative to single-head attention with the full dimension, because the per-head dimensionality is proportionally reduced, yet it significantly enriches the model's capacity to represent complex dependencies.

Because the Transformer processes all positions in parallel and contains no recurrence or convolution, it has no inherent mechanism for encoding the order of tokens in a sequence. Without positional information, the model would treat a sequence as a bag of tokens, unable to distinguish "the cat sat on the mat" from "the mat sat on the cat." To resolve this, the authors introduced positional encoding, which injects position-dependent signals into the input embeddings before they enter the attention layers. The original Transformer used sinusoidal positional encodings, defined by sine and cosine functions of different frequencies across the embedding dimensions. Specifically, for a position pos and dimension i, the encoding uses sin(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model)) for even dimensions and cos(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model)) for odd dimensions. This scheme was chosen because it provides a unique encoding for each position, and the sinusoidal form allows the model to learn to attend to relative positions, since the encoding of any fixed offset can be expressed as a linear function of the encoding at a given position. Later work explored learned positional embeddings and relative positional encodings such as rotary position embeddings, but the sinusoidal approach remains a foundational technique.

The complete Transformer architecture consists of an encoder and a decoder, each composed of stacked layers. Every encoder layer contains a multi-head self-attention sublayer followed by a position-wise feed-forward network, with residual connections and layer normalization applied around each sublayer. The decoder mirrors this structure but adds a cross-attention sublayer that attends to the encoder's output, and it masks future positions in its self-attention to preserve autoregressive generation. This encoder-decoder design was originally proposed for machine translation, where the encoder processes the source sentence and the decoder generates the target sentence token by token.

The impact of the Transformer has been profound and far-reaching. In natural language processing, the architecture gave rise to BERT, which uses only the encoder stack and is pretrained with masked language modeling and next-sentence prediction, achieving state-of-the-art results across a broad range of understanding tasks. The GPT family, by contrast, uses only the decoder stack and is trained autoregressively, scaling to hundreds of billions of parameters and demonstrating emergent capabilities in reasoning, code generation, and in-context learning. Beyond language, the Transformer has been adapted to computer vision through Vision Transformers, which treat image patches as tokens and apply self-attention over spatial positions, rivaling and often surpassing convolutional neural networks on image classification benchmarks. Transformers have also been applied to protein structure prediction, music generation, robotic control, and multimodal tasks that integrate text, images, and audio. The self-attention mechanism's ability to capture arbitrary pairwise interactions without architectural bias toward locality or sequentiality has proven to be a remarkably general-purpose computational primitive, and ongoing research continues to refine its efficiency through sparse attention, linear attention approximations, and hardware-aware implementations to extend its applicability to ever longer sequences and larger-scale problems.
