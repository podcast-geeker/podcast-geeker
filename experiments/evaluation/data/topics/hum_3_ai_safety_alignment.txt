The alignment problem—ensuring that artificial intelligence systems behave in accordance with human values, intentions, and ethical norms—has moved from the margins of AI research into its central discourse as models have grown dramatically more capable. Early concerns about alignment were largely theoretical, situated in philosophical thought experiments about superintelligent agents pursuing misspecified goals. Today, alignment is an immediate practical concern: large language models deployed to hundreds of millions of users can generate harmful content, reinforce biases, follow malicious instructions, or pursue unintended objectives, and each of these failure modes represents a tangible risk. The field of AI safety has responded with a rapidly evolving toolkit of techniques aimed at steering model behavior, detecting failure modes, and establishing robust safeguards, though significant open problems remain.

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, commonly known as RLHF, has become the dominant paradigm for aligning large language models with human preferences. The process typically unfolds in three stages. First, a pretrained language model undergoes supervised fine-tuning on a curated dataset of high-quality demonstrations, teaching the model to produce responses in a desired format and style. Second, human annotators compare pairs of model outputs for the same prompt and indicate which response they prefer, and these preference judgments are used to train a reward model—a separate neural network that predicts a scalar reward score reflecting human preferences. Third, the language model is further fine-tuned using Proximal Policy Optimization to maximize the reward predicted by the reward model while remaining close to the supervised fine-tuned policy through a Kullback-Leibler divergence penalty that prevents the model from degenerating into incoherent text. This pipeline, pioneered by OpenAI in the development of InstructGPT and subsequently refined for GPT-4, has proven remarkably effective at producing models that are more helpful, less harmful, and more honest. However, RLHF has significant limitations: the process is expensive and labor-intensive, reward models can be exploited through reward hacking where the model learns to produce outputs that score highly without genuinely satisfying preferences, and human preferences are themselves noisy, inconsistent, and subject to cultural variation, making the reward signal an imperfect proxy for true alignment.

Anthropic's Constitutional AI represents an alternative approach that reduces dependence on human feedback by enabling the model to self-supervise its alignment using explicitly stated principles, or a constitution. The model generates responses, then evaluates its own outputs against the constitutional principles, identifying potential violations such as harmful content, dishonesty, or ethical transgressions. It then revises its responses to address these critiques, producing improved outputs. This critique-revision cycle can be iterated multiple times, progressively refining the response. The resulting revised dataset is used for further training through supervised fine-tuning or reinforcement learning using the model's own constitutional judgments as the reward signal. Constitutional AI offers scalability advantages over RLHF by reducing annotator dependence, transparency through the explicit constitution, and adaptability through principle modification rather than new data collection.

Direct Preference Optimization, proposed by Rafailov et al. in 2023, has emerged as a compelling simplification of the RLHF pipeline. DPO eliminates the separate reward model and the complexities of reinforcement learning by reformulating the alignment objective as a classification problem over preference pairs. The key insight is that the optimal policy under the RLHF objective can be expressed in closed form as a function of the preference data and the reference policy, enabling direct optimization without the instabilities and computational overhead of PPO. DPO has demonstrated performance competitive with RLHF across multiple benchmarks while being substantially simpler to implement and more stable to train, leading to rapid adoption in the open-source model development community.

Red-teaming—the practice of systematically probing AI systems for vulnerabilities, failure modes, and harmful behaviors—has become an essential component of the AI safety toolkit. Drawing on traditions from cybersecurity and military strategy, AI red-teaming involves human testers or automated systems attempting to elicit undesirable outputs through adversarial prompting. Human red-teamers craft creative, contextually nuanced attacks exploiting edge cases and alignment blind spots. Automated red-teaming scales this process by using language models themselves to generate adversarial prompts, creating a feedback loop in which one model attacks and another defends. Major laboratories including OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and Meta have invested heavily in red-teaming programs, and the practice is increasingly recognized as a necessary step before deployment, analogous to penetration testing in software security.

Jailbreak attacks represent a specific and particularly concerning category of adversarial inputs designed to circumvent safety training. Prompt injection embeds instructions within user input that override the model's system prompt or safety guidelines. Role-playing exploits ask the model to adopt an unconstrained persona, effectively bypassing alignment training. Encoding tricks exploit the model's ability to process unusual formats—base64 encoding, pig Latin, or character-by-character spelling—to smuggle harmful requests past content filters. Multi-turn manipulation gradually escalates conversation content across many individually innocuous turns until the model is contextually primed for harmful outputs. The dynamic between jailbreak attacks and defenses has intensified as more sophisticated alignment prevents simple jailbreaks but creates incentives for more creative attack strategies.

Guardrails represent the complementary defensive infrastructure deployed around AI models to prevent harmful inputs and outputs. These include input classifiers that screen user prompts for prohibited content before reaching the model, output classifiers that evaluate responses against safety criteria before delivery, system prompt protections that define behavioral boundaries, and monitoring systems that detect patterns of adversarial usage. Modern architectures employ defense in depth, combining lightweight keyword filters with neural classifiers and the model's own chain-of-thought safety reasoning, where it explicitly deliberates about a request's safety implications before generating a response.

Perhaps the most profound open problem in AI safety is scalable oversight: ensuring AI systems remain aligned as they become more capable than the humans supervising them. Current alignment techniques depend fundamentally on human ability to evaluate outputs—judging preferences in RLHF, assessing constitutional violations, or determining whether red-team attacks succeeded. As AI approaches or exceeds human-level performance in specialized domains, evaluators may lack the expertise to reliably assess behavior. Proposed approaches include recursive reward modeling, where AI assists humans in evaluating other AI systems; debate frameworks, where two AI systems argue opposing positions for a human judge; and interpretability research aimed at making model internals transparent for direct inspection. The alignment community remains divided on the urgency of these challenges, but broad consensus holds that the current toolkit, while effective for present-day systems, will require fundamental extensions as capabilities advance.
